Visual Design Lecture 26/03/2020

Visual Elements in Design

Visual Design can be broken down into seven core principles:

  1. Lines
  2. Colours
  3. Shapes
  4. Textures
  5. Space
  6. Form
  7. Typography

I will describe each principle in more detail in each of the modules below.

  • Lines

    Lines

    The most basic visual element. While lines are plain, they are important core elements of design. Lines help you compose layouts and give structure to your information. They can add borders and frames, be used for decoration or as connectors or dividers of a layout. Lines can add prominence and organization to the elements of your design. Lines themselves come in many different forms: solid, dashed, thin, thick, straight, or curved.

    Video from Gareth David explaining Lines

  • Colours
    There is much to know about colour beyond the 12-colour wheel representations of primary (in light-based colour mixing, Red, Green, and Blue (or RGB) are most efficient for adding colours on a screen, making up the additive colour wheel), secondary, and tertiary colours. Besides RGB, there are some other important colour spaces and models: L*a*b*/LAB (either CIELAB [newer] or Hunter Lab [older], where in the former colour is represented in three dimensions: The L-axis to determine lightness from black to white, a*-axis from green to red/magenta, and the b*-axis from blue to yellow light), RGB/light-based variants: Hue-Saturation-Value (HSV), Hue-Saturation-Lightness (HSL), Hue-Saturation-Intensity (HSI), Hue-Saturation-Luma (HSY), grayscale, (for printers) Cyan-Magenta-Yellow-Key (CMYK), which is a subtractive colour space (because all colours added would yield a black colour), (often in cameras) Luminosity-Red-Chroma-Blue-Chroma (YCrCb), and finally XYZ (used for understanding human colour perception.
