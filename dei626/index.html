Visual Design Lecture 26/03/2020

Visual Elements in Design

Visual Elements are the building blocks of graphic design and make up our visual designs. As designers, we choose, blend, rearrange, and build on these visual elements to create our designs. We arrange them on a surface to build a layout to convey our ideas. Core visual elements are present in all graphic designs and consist of the following:

  1. Lines
  2. Colours
  3. Shapes
  4. Textures
  5. Space
  6. Form
  7. Typography

Whatever the final work you produce with them, these elements are the core of all of your visual designs. I will describe each principle in more detail in each of the modules below.

  • Lines

    Lines

    A line is the first visual element of graphic design. Lines are the most basic visual elements of design. While lines are plain, they are important core elements of design and should not be underestimated. Lines are quite versatile and come in many different forms: solid, dashed, thin, thick, straight, or curved. They help you compose layouts and give structure to your information. They can add borders and frames, be used for decoration or as connectors or dividers of a layout. Lines can add prominence and organization to the elements of your design. They will help draw your eyes to specific points of a layout. In type, they can help structure paragraphs and headings. In infographics, they can connect several elements of the information together and give it all a flow.

    If you think about magazines, newspapers, or maps, the line is the most common visual element found there because it works extremely well to structure much information. The following picture shows some variations of horizontal lines. The strokes are shown in different sizes and with hatching and sketching and other alternatives to display the shape of a line. Lines can consist of many individual strokes or dots.

    The following example is from Gareth David's course on the visual elements of graphic design. He composed the examples in black and white for the sake of simplicity. In the image below, the line is the foundation of building the infographic. It shows a tree diagram with lines coloured in different shades of gray. The visual icons in the leaves of the tree are also lines that make up the icons for illustration. It's a simple visual depiction of lines forming a tree with another line on the bottom to provide an anchor point for the eye and the layout to sit upon.

    The following is an example of line usage in typography. Here lines are used to draw emphasis to specific words.

    Below is an example of lines to create a border of a layout. There are two stroke-lines (thin and thick) on the edges of the layout that create this border or frame of the image. The type of stroke lines in relationship to the other elements of your composition define its look and feel. This case has a boutique feel to it. The line in the middle also elegantly divides the two types of the type present in the image.

    In the next example, lines are used to structure the composition of the layout. At the same time, the lines in the image decorate it. Shown is a chapter index page, the same way you would find in a book. We have a large dark line running vertically down the page and smaller lines dividing the content on the page horizontally. When the horizontal lines pass the vertical one, they are inverting their colour. This creates a loose grid for the type elements to exist. The lines are clearly defining and position the type elements on the page.

    Here is another example of how lines can divide information on a page. Newspapers struggle with presenting a complex structure of information to their readers. Much information is presented at the same time. Lines play a core role here to divide and decorate the information. At the top, the lines separate the section header from the rest of the article below. In the top section and the bottom section we also have small lines to call out and decorate the information. Lines do not always have to be represented as solid strokes but also come from contrasts of colours or shape. For example, there is a big picture in the article and the solid colour fill of that picture creates a line around it. In the main body of text, we have an infographic again made with lines. Some lines break information out, in particular diagrams, quotes, and footers of the article.

    The last example shows lines creating decoration. You can see a variety of lines embellishing the page. First, you see lines on the page creating decoration by using different stroke effects on them. Those two larger sets of lines on the top left and the bottom right also create a larger white background line in the middle of the page (as negative space). There are also three columns of type visible with the third column breaking out of the alignment and up to the top. This creates an interesting and dynamic layout on the page.

    There are many ways that lines can be used in graphic design and these are just some core examples. Train your eye when you look at graphic design to see how lines have been used as graphic elements in the design to create interesting layouts.

  • Colours

    Colour can create emphasis, give impact, and build a specific look and feel in graphic design. When working with colour, it helps to know a bit about colour theory. It all starts with the colour wheel. This example here is the red-yellow-blue colour model consisting of 12 colours. A colour wheel can also be seen as a colour spectrum. If you want to experiment a bit more with colour, I recommend you try out Adobe Colour.

    There is much to know about colour beyond the 12-colour wheel representations of primary (in light-based colour mixing, Red, Green, and Blue (or RGB) are most efficient for adding colours on a screen, making up the additive colour wheel), secondary, and tertiary colours.

    Besides RGB, there are some other important colour spaces and models:

    • L*a*b*/LAB (either CIELAB [newer] or Hunter Lab [older], where in the former colour is represented in three dimensions: The L-axis to determine lightness from black to white, a*-axis from green to red/magenta, and the b*-axis from blue to yellow light)
    • RGB/light-based variants:
      • Hue-Saturation-Value (HSV)
      • Hue-Saturation-Lightness (HSL)
      • Hue-Saturation-Intensity (HSI)
      • Hue-Saturation-Luma (HSY)
    • Grayscale, (for printers)
    • Cyan-Magenta-Yellow-Key (CMYK), which is a subtractive colour space (because all colours added would yield a black colour)
    • Luminosity-Red-Chroma-Blue-Chroma (YCrCb) (often in cameras)
    • XYZ (used for understanding human colour perception)

    Video from Gareth David explaining Colour

  • Shapes

    Shapes

    Everything is ultimately a shape. All objects on your screen or page are shapes. Shapes have two dimensions: height and width.

    They are defined by boundaries such as lines or colour. Shapes can also be created with negative space. Shapes are used to add interested and substance to graphic design. You can decorate with them, use them as symbols, for patterns, or textures.

    There are two types of shapes: geometric shapes and organic shapes. Geometric shapes can be drawn with a ruler or circle of mainly straight lines. They produce a feeling of control or order. Organic shapes can be drawn freehand or are based on shapes found in nature. They produce a natural feeling.

    Shapes are single visual elements, so in visual design, we combine shapes and place them together to create visual relationships. This relationship, be it a simple or complex one, can trigger feelings, convey messages, or engage your audience, it adds emphasis to a portion of your layouts and creates the perception of movement. Grouping or moving shapes closer or further apart from one another, we tap into the C.R.A.P. design principle of proximity, discussed below. You can combine geometric and organic shapes and fuse them on many levels to create a variety of design elements that allows you to communicate visually.
